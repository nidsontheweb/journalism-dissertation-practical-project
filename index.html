Etchings on the wall

How Glasgow graffiti fits within the global culture and what it has done for the city itself.

Graffiti is a style of art that has permeated society for centuries. It’s defined by the Cambridge dictionary as “words or drawings, especially humorous, rude, or political, on walls, doors, etc. in public places” . The art can range from a simple ‘tag’ or name to a large, carefully planned and colourful design. Walls are an artist’s chosen medium here, but wall art can be called street art, graffiti or mural painting. The distinction can often lie in the legalities . Graffiti’s status in this respect has remained usually illegal, created without permission, especially in history. Murals are also public art, usually on walls or ceilings, but are different because they are legal, accepted or allowed.

“This is generational,” says Gaz Mac aka Gary Mackay, the Studio Director of Yardworks Glasgow , “from ancient Europe, Ancient Africa, Ancient Asia.” Yardworks started as a simple arts initiative, rising into a creative programme recognised and respected globally. It’s located in the same compound as music venue, SWG3 and has not only become synonymous with championing the best of Glasgow graffiti but pulled artists from across the globe to paint in Glasgow, hosting a massive annual graffiti festival. Mac conceived of the venue in 2004 alongside SWG3 founder Andrew Fleming-Brown. “There was always graffiti on castles and caves.”

What caves does Gaz Mac speak of? This comment he made – at the very beginning of our conversation – made me pause while Mac, speaking a mile a minute, had moved on to Glasgow’s introduction to graffiti through local gangs – but more on the gangs later.

Some official reports of archaeology done in sites like Lascaux, France and Altamira, Spain with cave paintings do refer to “graffiti-like scenes” of sex and hunting . Art historians have even noticed many the handprints of many teenage males dominating the marks left on the caves. Others voice their disdain toward making the mistake of crediting the art of these ancient humans as graffiti . Jeffery Ian Ross says the premise is flawed, since we do not know if the markings were made without the permission of the one living or working at the cave. It can only be graffiti if an expert says so according to Ian Ross - an expert, peer-reviewed one - as the lived experience of a graffiti tagger may not amount to any knowledge still. But academic literature refers to, say the negative handprints from prehistoric times, or names (tags), handprints, portrait sketches etc. as personal identifiers in history . While debate on what is and isn’t graffiti is just as rife as conflicts over whether this art should be legal or not, these impressions are personal identifiers and tell us about the hand responsible for making the art.

This is what Polly Lohmann argues. Ancient graffiti can be found on long corridors in Pompeii, preserved by the ash of Mount Vesuvius when it erupted . Despite textual evidence showing us inscriptions at these sites were banned, they can be found on Roman graves or medieval churches, which usually survive since these sites are kept in continuous use or are protected as sacred spaces and cultural heritage (p. 41). The inscriptions were usually names, personal symbols and images. “The language and style of graffiti can reveal different levels of literacy,” she says, “local dialects, common forms of orthography or the relation between oral and written languages,” (p. 45).

This instinct, it seems, recurs throughout history. Indeed, Glasgow is not new to this phenomenon. Atlas Obscura reported on “vandalistic inscriptions” dating back to the early nineteenth century, at the Cathedral of St. Mungo or Glasgow Cathedral, a combination of names, initials, dates and a series of sentences . While some simply choose to say, “Die Wee Must”, others have carved out combinations of hymns, poem or Bible excepts.

“By containing personal identifiers, self-reflective jokes, personal messages and statements, graffiti represent the individual rather than the whole of society…emotions rather than facts and therefore, have a specific value as historical sources,” (Lohmann, 2020, p. 51). Graffiti, or etchings on a wall are washed off easily, a consequence of being exposed to the natural elements. If aliens end up finding a desolate Earth, perhaps, the art that remained might paint them a picture of our lives. Returning to the present, the original taggers of New York were well aware of the fragile nature of their art. So why do they, or any graffiti artist in the US, Brazil or Glasgow, still do it?

Subway Art

The form and style of modern graffiti originated in New York, inspired by burgeoning hip-hop of the time. It grew alongside new music and DJs, dance forms like breaking and popping . While the graffiti before 1965 was gang-related with members interested in marking territory on walls, it was separate from the writing of the 70s and 80s. Here, “dispossessed young people” were starting to make art out of their frustration and boredom (p. 31) . Writers – as they preferred to be called – like Taki 183 wrote their name everywhere, stepping up to ‘tagging’ trains and buildings eventually . Experimenting with style and size, these youth chose subway cars as their ideal medium since the trains would travel – and thus display their name – all over the city (p. 14-15).

Yardworks boss Gaz Mac grew up in Govan, Glasgow and was exposed to graffiti at twelve years old. The chosen mode of transport for young boys here, though, were the backs of old corporation buses. Mac wrote for the Young Tiny or the Young Teens, a crew it seems of boys who would tag big ‘Y’s in a sans-serif font style . Housing estates and an abundance of drugs is the setting he describes, a child of sixteen around addicts in the depths of a heroin epidemic. The jilted generation, he said he belonged to, with “Margaret Thacher coming into power, the Tories, and the jobs,” which he describes as closing down in all the major Glaswegian industries – shipbuilding, coalmines, steel plants. The youth were put into opportunity programs.

Mac stood out in this city in his younger years, adopting hip-hop culture among the punks and rockers, and making it his own. “Very British,” is how he described it along with UK music and graffiti figures Goldie, the jungle producer, and 3D from trip hop band Massive Attack. He’d travel down to Manchester or Liverpool to spray paint, “Every now and then I'd meet somebody. At that age, nobody took you serious. 'It's just some Scots guy. Here's this little jock, says he's doing graffiti.'” Boys have a way to make you feel like a wee, little human. The taunts only made Mac more determined. “The more you done it, the better you got. The more respect you got,” he describes. This philosophy of quantity and/or quality earning respect is talked about in Nancy Macdonald’s book, ‘The graffiti subculture: youth, masculinity, and identity in London and New York’.

Since graffiti is (or was since the legal status of graffiti is changing in cities like Glasgow) largely illegal, the act can be perceived as masculine by the boys that run in this subculture. They compare the daring endeavours, – a particularly hard-to-access train, or a stylish ‘piece’ in a big or visible spot – weigh the risks, respect the machismo. This is why boys may feel like they need to enact their masculinity – the boldness of graffiti and the risks with authorities that used to be involved – over and over again, as “no sooner is it proved that it is again questioned,” according to Macdonald (p. 105).

Showing and upholding respect is important in the subculture. “I was referred to the wee graffiti guy. 'Oh, the wee graffiti guy'll do that,'” Gaz Mac recalled of a time one year ago, a graffiti veteran of almost 45 years. “The wee graffiti guy will not do that,” he sasses, “And if the wee graffiti guy does that, he'll charge you a fortune for it.” Graffiti is built on an intricate hierarchy, and members are not afraid to quality control.
